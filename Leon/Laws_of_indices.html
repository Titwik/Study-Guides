Laws of indices

Author

Zheng Chen

Summary
The laws of indices enable expressions involving powers to be manipulated more efficiently than writing them out in full.

Before reading this guide, it is recommended that you read the guides on ‘Logarithms’, as well as ‘Dealing with powers and nth roots’.

What are indices?

Indices are used to display how many times a number has been multiplied by itself. They can also be used to represent roots and fractions, such as the square root. The laws of indices make it possible to alter expressions involving powers more quickly than if they were written out whole.

This guide will introduce six properties of indices: multiplication, division, brackets, power of zero, negative indices, fractional indices and Euler’s number: \(e\).

Definition of an index

A number or a variable may have an index. Index of a variable (or a constant) is a value that is raised to the power of the variable. The indices are also known as powers or exponents. It shows the number of times a given number has to be multiplied. It is represented in the form:

\[ a^m = a· a· a · \ ... \ · a (m \ times)\]

Here, \(a\) is the base and \(m\) is the index.

Example 1

\[2^5 = 2·2·2·2·2 = 32\]

In this example, \(2\) is the base and \(3\) is the index.

Laws of indices

Law 1: Multiplication

If the two terms have the same base (in this case \(a\)) and are to be multiplied together their indices are added (in this case \(m+n\)):

\[a^m·a^n = a^{m+n}\] For example: \[3^2·3^3 = 9·27 = 243 = 3^5 = 3^{2+3}\]

Example 2

Simplify the following expression: \(x^2y^3· x^3y^{-1}\)

You can treat \(x\) and \(y\) as two separate terms and you are now simplifying \(x^2·x^3\) and \(y^3·y^{-1}\). Using Law 1, you can add the indices: \(x^{2+3}\) and \(y^{3+(-1)}\). Remember to multiply \(x\) and \(y\) terms, then you can get: \[x^2y^3· x^3y^{-1} = x^{2+3}y^{3-1} = x^5y^2\]

Law 2: Division

If the two terms have the same base (in this case \(a\)) and are to be divided their indices are subtracted (in this case \(m-n\)):

\[\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}\] For example: \[\frac{2^5}{2^3} = 4 = 2^2 = 2^{5-3}\]

Example 3

Simplify the following expression: \(\frac{x^2y^3}{x^5y}\)

You can treat \(\frac{x^2y^3}{x^5y}\) as the product of two fractions which are \(\frac{x^2}{x^5}\) and \(\frac{y^3}{y}\). Using Law 2, you can simplify the fractions and get \(\frac{x^2}{x^5} = x^{2-5} = x^{-3}\) and \(\frac{y^3}{y} = y^{3-1} = y^2\), remember to multiply the fractions and you can get: \[\frac{x^2y^3}{x^5y} = x^{2-5}y^{3-1} = x^{-3}y^2\]

Law 3: Brackets

To raise a value or variable (in this case \(a\)) presented in index form to another index, multiply the indices together (\(m\) and \(n\)):

\[(a^m)^n = a^{m·n}\] For example: \[(3^2)^3 = 729 = 3^6 = 3^{2·3}\]

Example 4

Simplify the following expression: \((x^2y^3)^4\)

Using Law 3, you can get \((x^2y^3)^4 = (x^2)^4·(y^3)^4 = x^{2·4}y^{3·4} = x^8y^{12}\)

Law 4: Power of 0

Anything to the power of zero is equal to one:

\[a^0 = 1\]

Example 5

Simplify the following expression: \((x^2 + y^5)^0\)

Using Law 4, you can get: \((x^2 + y^5)^0 = 1\)

Example 6

\[0^0 = 1\]

see Zero to the power of zero for more explanation.

Proof of Law 4

Law 2 helps to explain Law 4:

Since anything divided by itself is equal to one, so \[\frac{a^m}{a^m} = 1\]

Also

\[\frac{a^m}{a^m} = a^{m-m} = a^0 = 1\] Therefore, \[a^0 = 1\]

Note

Anything to the power of one is equal to itself \[a^1 = a\]

\(1\) to the power of anything is \(1\)

Law 5: Negative indices

If the index is a negative value, then it can be shown as the reciprocal of the positive index raised to the same variable:

\[a^{-m} = \frac{1}{a^m}\]

For example: \[2^{-2} = (2^2)^{-1} = 4^{-1} = \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{2^2}\]

In this case, Law 3 is used to simplify the base \(2^2\) to \(4\), then Law 5 is applied.

Alternatively,

\[2^{-2} = 2^{0-2} = \frac{2^0}{2^2} = \frac{1}{2^2}\]

In this case, Law 4 is used to re-express the index and Law 2 is used.

Example 7

Simplify the following expression: \((3x+4y)^{-1}\)

Using Law 5, you can get: \((3x+4y)^{-1} = \frac{1}{3x+4y}\)

Law 6: Fractional indices

An index in the form of a fraction can be represented as the radical form:

\[a^{\frac{m}{n}} = (\sqrt[n]{a})^m\] For example: \[2^{\frac{3}{5}} = \sqrt[5]{2^3} = \sqrt[5]{8}\]

Example 8

Simplify \(27^{\frac{-2}{3}}\)

Using Law 7, you can get \(27^{\frac{-2}{3}} = (\sqrt[3]{27})^{-2} = 3^{-2} = \frac{1}{3^2} = \frac{1}{9}\)

Solving equations using indices

You can use laws of indices to solve equations, here are some examples:

Example 9

Solve \(x^{\frac{1}{2}}=8\)

Start by squaring both sides of the equation: \((x^{\frac{1}{2}})^2 = 8^2\), you can get the answer\(x = 64\). To check if the answer is correct, you can substitute \(64\) into the equation:\(64^{\frac{1}{2}} = \sqrt[2]{64}=8\).

Example 10

Solve \(x^{-2}=9\)

\(x^{-2} = 9\) can be re-expressed as \(\frac{1}{x^2} = 9\), multiply both sides by \(x^2\) gives you \(1=9x^2\), then divide both sides by \(9\) gives you \(\frac{1}{9}=x^2\). Now you can square root both sides \(\sqrt[2]{\frac{1}{9}}=x\), but remember you could have positive or negative roots. Therefore, you can get \(x= \frac{1}{3}\) or \(x= -\frac{1}{3}\).

Example 11

Solve \(3^{4x}=27^{5-x}\)

Re-write the equation as \(3^{4x}=(3^3)^{5-x}\), then \(3^{4x}=3^{15-3x}\) using the laws of indices. Since both sides are equal, \(4x=15-3x\), then \(x=\frac{15}{7}\).

Introduction to \(e\)

Euler’s number

Euler’s number, denoted \(e\), is a constant approximately equal to \(2.71828\). It is the base of natural logarithms. It can be also calculated as the sum of the infinite series

\[e = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} = 1+\frac{1}{1!}+\frac{1}{2!}+\frac{1}{3!}+ \cdots\]

Example

Solve \(e^{3x}=12\)

Take logarithm of both side gives you \(\log_e(e^{3x})=\log_e(12)\). \(\log_e(x)\) is the natural logarithm often denoted \(\ln(x)\), so you can write the equation as \(\ln(e^{3x})=\ln(12)\). Using the power rule of logarithm, you can express the equation as \(3x \cdot \ln(e) = \ln(12)\), then by the identity rule, you can get \(3x \cdot (1)=\ln(12)\). Rearrange the equation gives you \(x=\frac{\ln(12)}{3}\).
